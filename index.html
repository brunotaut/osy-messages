Any drone — even a recreational one — entering the approach corridor forces an immediate halt to all landing and takeoff operations. One hour of closure at a busy airport costs millions in grounded aircraft, crew disruption, and passenger compensation. The cost of a single incident routinely exceeds the annual licence fee for a protection system.

A $500 FPV drone carrying a small payload can destroy a $2 million vehicle or disable critical base infrastructure. Modern conflict has demonstrated that small commercial drones cause damage far beyond their cost. Bases without autonomous aerial defense are exposed continuously — attackers choose their moment, and human sentries cannot maintain vigilance around the clock.

Detected at distance, classified as strike threat, intercepted before reaching the target perimeter. Zero dependence on GPS or network connectivity.

Prisons

Drone-based contraband delivery is now the primary method for getting drugs, phones, and weapons into prisons across Europe. Each successful delivery undermines facility control, enables criminal coordination from inside, and exposes management to regulatory and legal liability. Walls, cameras, and guards have no answer to a drone arriving from above at night.

Drone detected on approach, intercepted before reaching the delivery point. Package never arrives.

Government & Border Security

Government facilities and border zones are targets because of what they represent — authority, intelligence, and national sovereignty. A surveillance drone over an embassy or a ministry doesn't need to carry a weapon: the intelligence it collects can be worth more than any physical asset. At borders, undetected aerial crossings — whether carrying payloads or mapping sensor gaps — have consequences that compound over time.

Detected at the boundary, trajectory modelled, intercepted before penetrating protected territory.

The cost of a prolonged outage, measured in economic disruption, emergency response, and infrastructure repair, runs into the hundreds of millions. Physical strikes on energy infrastructure are immediate and very difficult to reverse.

A drone over the port basin can map container positions, cargo schedules, and security patterns in minutes — intelligence that enables theft, smuggling, or a targeted strike. The disruption of a key port, even for 24 hours, cascades through supply chains across entire industries and countries.

A drone strike doesn't just damage equipment: it triggers environmental incidents, regulatory investigations, and supply disruptions that take months and hundreds of millions to resolve. The remote footprint makes conventional security impractical.

Aerial surveillance of production processes is also a growing vector for industrial espionage.

Frequently Asked Questions

Technical &
Procurement Questions

Detailed answers for infrastructure operators, security integrators, and procurement teams.

Most counter-drone systems stop at detection — they alert an operator that a drone is present, and a human decides what to do. Laplace Dome closes the entire loop autonomously. It detects the threat, classifies its type, models its trajectory, dispatches an interceptor, and executes terminal guidance without waiting for manual activation. In a fast-moving threat scenario, the difference between detection-only and autonomous response is the difference between watching the threat arrive and neutralising it before it reaches the perimeter. The operator retains full visibility and override capability throughout.
OSY Fly does not manufacture sensors or UAVs. Laplace Dome is a software intelligence layer that integrates with partner hardware — existing radar installations, third-party camera arrays, and any capable UAV interceptor platform. You are not locked into a proprietary sensor ecosystem, and you can integrate Laplace Dome with infrastructure security systems you already own. It also means we can adapt to new sensor types and effector platforms as the threat landscape evolves, without replacing the core software stack.
No. All critical computation runs on local edge hardware. There is no cloud dependency in the detection, classification, fusion, or guidance pipeline. Infrastructure sites cannot assume communications will be intact during an attack, and any latency introduced by network round-trips is incompatible with the response times required. The system can receive software updates and transmit incident logs when connectivity is available, but these are non-critical functions that do not affect operational performance.
Laplace Dome is designed specifically for GPS-denied and electronically contested environments. Lagrange does not rely on external positioning signals for terminal guidance. Navigation and intercept logic are derived from sensor data processed locally by Riemann and FusionFactory. This architecture emerged directly from operational experience in active conflict zones where GPS denial and spoofing are routine tactics. It is not an optional resilience feature — it is a core design requirement.
FusionFactory AI maintains independent track objects for each detected target simultaneously, each with its own position, speed vector, classification confidence score, and predicted trajectory. Lagrange coordinates multi-intercept sequencing — prioritising threats by type, proximity, and vector — and dispatches available interceptor assets accordingly. A swarm attack scenario involving simultaneous multiple vectors is a design case, not an edge case.
Riemann achieves a verified hit rate exceeding 90% in daylight conditions, with object recognition down to 30 pixels. False positive management is a primary design concern for civil infrastructure deployment. FusionFactory's cross-sensor validation is the key mechanism: a threat is only escalated to the response layer when confirmed across multiple independent sensor modalities. Classification by Riemann provides a confidence score; FusionFactory provides cross-sensor corroboration. Both conditions must be met before autonomous response is triggered.
No. The system is designed for 24/7 autonomous operation with minimal human oversight. Detection, classification, fusion, and intercept execution all occur without requiring a human to be watching a screen. Operators typically configure rules of engagement during setup — specifying which threat classifications trigger automatic response and which require confirmation — and receive real-time alerts and post-event reports. The operator is always in command and can override at any point, but their presence is not required for the site to be protected.
Yes. Riemann and Lagrange are both commercially deployable as standalone modules and form OSY Fly's primary commercial software offering. They can be licensed and integrated onto third-party UAV platforms, embedded in existing counter-UAS systems, or deployed as the intelligence core of a partner's own product. Laplace Dome is the integrated reference architecture; Riemann and Lagrange are the modules that power it, available independently at license.
Riemann is trained and validated against FPV drones, loitering munitions, reconnaissance UAVs, and tactical unmanned platforms. The classification model outputs a category and confidence score for each detected object. Training data comes from real operational scenarios — including active conflict environments — rather than simulation alone. The Itō environment enables continuous retraining as new drone types and tactics emerge.
Laplace Dome integrates with electro-optical cameras, radar, acoustic sensors, and RF monitoring systems. No specific vendor or model is required — the system's hardware-agnostic architecture accepts data from any capable sensor via standard interfaces. For a minimum viable deployment, an EO camera with Riemann provides detection and classification. Adding radar and acoustic inputs through FusionFactory progressively improves confidence, reduces false positives, and extends effective range.
The Itō simulation and training environment replicates the flight dynamics, sensor signatures, and behavioural patterns of known and novel drone types in a high-fidelity virtual environment. New models are trained in simulation, stress-tested against synthetic adversarial scenarios, and validated via SITL and HITL pipelines before deployment to live systems. This enables OSY Fly to update Riemann's classification capability as new threats emerge — without requiring live-fire testing for each iteration.
For Riemann or Lagrange integration onto an existing UAV platform, timelines are measured in weeks. For a full Laplace Dome deployment at a fixed site with sensor integration and rules-of-engagement configuration, typical timelines are discussed during the assessment phase. We engage with operators and integrators early in the procurement process to define the sensor complement, network topology, and operational parameters before any hardware is specified.
